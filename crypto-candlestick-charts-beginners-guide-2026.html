What Is a Candlestick Chart?

A candlestick chart is a style of financial chart used to describe price movements of an asset over time. Each "candle" represents a specific time period and shows four critical pieces of information:

  • Open â€” The price at the beginning of the time period
  • Close â€” The price at the end of the time period
  • High â€” The highest price reached during the period
  • Low â€” The lowest price reached during the period

Anatomy of a Single Candle

Every candlestick has two main parts:

The Body (the thick rectangular part):

  • Green (or white) body = Price went UP during the period (Close > Open). This is called a bullish candle.
  • Red (or black) body = Price went DOWN during the period (Close < Open). This is called a bearish candle.

The Wick (or Shadow) (the thin lines above and below the body):

  • Upper wick = Highest price reached before sellers pushed price back down
  • Lower wick = Lowest price reached before buyers pushed price back up

The relationship between the body size and wick length tells you about market momentum and conviction. A candle with a large body and tiny wicks shows strong directional momentum. A candle with a tiny body and long wicks shows indecision or rejection of a price level.

Quick Memory Trick: Green candle = Bulls in control (price up). Red candle = Bears in control (price down). Long wicks = The other side fought back. Long body with short wicks = The winning side was dominant.

Support and Resistance: The Foundation of Technical Analysis

Beyond individual candles, the most important concept in technical analysis is support and resistance:

  • Support â€” A price level where buying pressure is strong enough to stop a downtrend. Think of it as a "floor" under the price.
  • Resistance â€” A price level where selling pressure is strong enough to stop an uptrend. Think of it as a "ceiling" above the price.

How to Identify Support and Resistance on Candlestick Charts

  1. Look for price "bounces" â€” Find levels where the price reversed multiple times (e.g., bounced up from $0.05 three times = support at $0.05)
  2. Identify previous highs and lows â€” Old resistance becomes new support after a breakout (and vice versa)
  3. Watch for "liquidity sweeps" â€” On meme coins, you'll often see the price briefly break below a support level (taking out stop-losses) and immediately reverse higher. This is called a "liquidity grab" and is extremely common in Solana meme coins

Top Technical Indicators for Meme Coin Traders

While candlestick patterns are powerful on their own, combining them with these three indicators will dramatically improve your meme coin trading:

1. Relative Strength Index (RSI)

The RSI measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a scale of 0 to 100:

  • RSI > 70 = Overbought (price may be due for a pullback)
  • RSI < 30 = Oversold (price may be due for a bounce)
  • RSI > 80 on meme coins = Can stay overbought during strong pumps â€” don't short just because RSI is high

Meme coin adaptation: Traditional RSI levels don't work perfectly for meme coins. Adjust your thresholds: consider RSI < 25 as extreme oversold and RSI > 85 as extreme overbought. Meme coins trend harder and longer than blue-chip cryptos.

2. Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)

The MACD shows the relationship between two moving averages of price:

  • MACD line crosses above signal line = Bullish signal (momentum shifting up)
  • MACD line crosses below signal line = Bearish signal (momentum shifting down)
  • MACD histogram turning positive = Bullish momentum increasing
  • MACD histogram turning negative = Bearish momentum increasing

Meme coin adaptation: Use shorter-period MACD settings (e.g., 6, 13, 5 instead of the default 12, 26, 9) to capture the faster moves of meme coins.

3. Moving Averages (MA)

Moving averages smooth out price data to identify trends:

  • 20-period MA = Short-term trend (5-minute chart: ~1.5 hours of data)
  • 50-period MA = Medium-term trend
  • 200-period MA = Long-term trend

Meme coin adaptation: The 20 EMA (Exponential Moving Average) on the 15-minute chart is the most closely watched moving average for Solana meme coin traders. Bounces off the 20 EMA are common entry signals during uptrends.

Common Mistakes New Meme Coin Chart Readers Make

Even with solid pattern knowledge, beginners often make these mistakes:

1. Over-analyzing on the Wrong Timeframe

Looking at 1-minute charts for trend analysis is like using a microscope to navigate a city. You'll see every tiny move but miss the overall direction. Always identify the trend on a higher timeframe before zooming in for entries.

2. Trading Every Pattern

This is the most expensive mistake in trading. If the trend is clearly up (higher highs and higher lows on the 4-hour chart), don't short just because you see a small bearish pattern. Trends are your friend; fight them at your own expense.

5. Not Using Stop-Losses

Meme coins can drop 50% in minutes. Without a stop-loss, a single bad trade can wipe out weeks of profits. Always set a stop-loss when you enter a trade, and move it to break-even once the trade moves in your favor.
