Solana DeFi Audit Guide: How to Read and Evaluate Smart Contract Audits Before Depositing Funds

Over $300 million was lost to Solana DeFi exploits in 2025. Smart contract audits are your first line of defense — but only if you know how to read them. Here's exactly how to evaluate audit reports like a security professional.

Updated: May 26, 2026|12 min read|DogeKing Security Team

You've found a promising Solana DeFi protocol offering 18% APY on USDC deposits. The website says "audited by Neodyme." You check the docs — there's a link to a PDF. You open it, skim the first page, see a lot of technical jargon, and close it. Good enough, right?

Wrong.

In 2025, Solana DeFi protocols suffered over $300 million in losses from smart contract exploits, according to data from Solscan and DefiLlama. Several of those hacked protocols had published audit reports. The audits weren't fake — they simply weren't as comprehensive as depositors assumed.

Smart contract audits are not a binary "safe" or "unsafe" stamp. They're technical documents with specific scope, severity classifications, acknowledged risks, and limitations. Knowing how to read them is a critical skill for anyone depositing funds into Solana DeFi protocols. This guide walks you through exactly how to evaluate an audit report before you commit a single dollar.

What Exactly Is a Smart Contract Audit?

A smart contract audit is a systematic review of a protocol's codebase by independent security researchers. The goal is to identify vulnerabilities, logic errors, economic exploits, and deviations from best practices before malicious actors find them.

Audits typically cover:

  • Manual code review — Security experts read every line of the smart contract source code line by line, looking for logical flaws, arithmetic errors, and edge cases that could be exploited.
  • Automated static analysis — Tools scan the codebase for known vulnerability patterns, such as reentrancy, integer overflow, missing access controls, and unsafe arithmetic.
  • Fuzz testing — Automated tools generate random or semi-random inputs to stress-test the contract and uncover unexpected behavior.
  • Economic/mathematical modeling — For DeFi protocols specifically, auditors model the economic incentives to ensure the math works correctly and can't be gamed.
  • Recommendations and remediation — Findings are classified by severity, and the protocol team is expected to fix or acknowledge each issue before the audit is considered complete.

Key point: An audit is a point-in-time assessment. It covers the code at a specific commit hash on a specific date. Any code changes made after the audit — including upgrades, bug fixes, or new features — fall outside the audit scope unless specifically re-audited.

Understanding what an audit doesn't cover is just as important. Audits generally do not cover front-end code, social engineering attacks, or private key compromises. A protocol might have a perfectly secure smart contract and still be exploited because its admin keys were stored insecurely or its front end was compromised via a DNS attack.

Top Solana Audit Firms: Neodyme, OtterSec, and Kudelski

Not all audit firms are created equal. On Solana, three firms have established themselves as the gold standard for smart contract security reviews. Understanding their reputations, methodologies, and track records helps you evaluate the weight of their audit reports.

🛡️ Neodyme

Reputation: Arguably the most respected Solana-native audit firm. Neodyme has conducted security reviews for some of the largest Solana DeFi protocols including Jupiter, Marinade Finance, and Solend. They are known for deep technical analysis and their own Solana-specific security tooling.

Strengths: Deep Solana expertise (they built Solana-specific static analysis tools), strong track record of finding critical vulnerabilities pre-launch, transparent reporting with clear severity classifications, and they offer retainer-based ongoing monitoring for protocols.

What to look for: Neodyme reports include a detailed scope section listing exactly which programs and functions were audited. They use a clear severity rating system (Critical/High/Medium/Low/Informational). A Neodyme audit with zero unresolved Critical or High findings carries significant weight.

🔬 OtterSec

Reputation: One of the most active Solana security firms, OtterSec has audited hundreds of Solana protocols and is known for publishing detailed post-mortems of major hacks. They're frequently hired by top-tier Solana projects and have a strong presence in the ecosystem.

Strengths: Extremely thorough reporting with quantitative risk assessment, strong focus on economic exploits in DeFi protocols, excellent public educational content (their hack post-mortems are industry standard), and they maintain a public dashboard of known vulnerabilities.

What to look for: OtterSec audits typically include a risk score, a detailed breakdown of each finding with code-level reproduction steps, and a clear status for each issue (Fixed, Acknowledged, or Partially Fixed). Pay attention to any "Acknowledged" findings — these are issues the protocol team chose not to fix, which means risk is accepted by users.

⚙️ Kudelski Security

Reputation: A well-established traditional cybersecurity firm (part of the Kudelski Group) that has expanded into blockchain security. They audit across multiple chains including Solana and bring enterprise-grade security methodology to DeFi audits.

Strengths: Enterprise-level rigor, extensive experience outside crypto (they audit everything from IoT to cloud infrastructure), formal verification capabilities, and multi-disciplinary teams that include cryptography experts, network security engineers, and smart contract specialists.

What to look for: Kudelski reports tend to be longer and more formal, with extensive appendices. They include detailed threat modeling sections and often provide recommendations beyond just code fixes (architectural improvements, operational security suggestions). Their findings are classified using CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System) standards, which can be helpful for cross-referencing severity.

Other notable Solana audit firms include Halborn (multichain, strong track record), Trail of Bits (prestigious but more expensive), Zokyo (growing Solana practice), Sec3 (Solana-focused), and Veridise (formal verification specialists). A protocol audited by two or more independent firms — especially if one of the top three is involved — is generally considered more trustworthy than one audited by a single lesser-known firm.

💡 Tip: Check if the audit firm has previously found critical vulnerabilities in other Solana protocols. A firm with a track record of discovering serious issues before launch is more valuable than one that's never found anything critical. You can verify this through audit report archives, DefiLlama's security dashboard, or the firms' own published reports.

How to Interpret Audit Report Findings: Critical, High, Medium, Low, Informational

The single most important skill in reading audit reports is understanding severity classifications. While firms may use slightly different terminology, the general hierarchy is consistent across the industry.

Severity Definition What It Means for You
Critical An exploit that can drain all or most protocol funds with minimal preconditions. Direct economic loss is certain if exploited. Dealbreaker. If any Critical finding is unresolved, do not deposit. If it's marked "Fixed," verify that a re-audit or at least a diff review was performed — don't trust single-line "fixed" markers.
High A vulnerability that can cause significant fund loss, permanent lockup, or complete protocol failure under specific conditions (e.g., oracle manipulation, price calculation errors, access control bypass). Major concern. Should be resolved (Fixed) and confirmed by the auditor. One or two High findings that were properly fixed are acceptable; several unresolved High findings signal systemic issues.
Medium A bug or design flaw that could cause limited fund loss, temporary lockup of assets, or unexpected behavior under edge cases (e.g., rounding errors, unscheduled liquidations, misconfigured parameters). Evaluate context. Medium findings are common and often acceptable if acknowledged with proper risk documentation. The key question: could this medium issue combine with another condition to become high-severity?
Low Minor issues that have minimal security impact — coding style violations, unused variables, missing events, gas optimization opportunities. Generally acceptable. Almost every audit has Low findings. They rarely pose direct financial risk but can indicate overall code quality.
Info Observations, best-practice suggestions, or informational notes that are not vulnerabilities but could improve code quality or user experience. Standard. Informational findings are a sign that the auditor was thorough. They show the protocol team is aware of potential improvements even if they aren't critical.

Red Flags in Audit Reports

Beyond the raw severity counts, watch for these specific warning signs when reading an audit report:

  • Too many "Acknowledged" findings — If a protocol team acknowledges a Critical or High finding without fixing it, that's a deliberate decision to accept the risk. You're accepting that risk when you deposit. Understand exactly what it means.
  • Narrow scope disclosure — The audit scope section should clearly state which specific programs, functions, and entry points were reviewed. If the scope is vague or only covers a small portion of the protocol (e.g., "only the staking contract, not the lending pool"), the audit is incomplete.
  • No remediated version provided — After findings are reported, the protocol should produce a remediated codebase that fixes the issues. A reputable audit report will link to both the original and remediated commit hashes so you can verify the fixes yourself.
  • Outdated audit — An audit from 2024 on a codebase that has been upgraded multiple times since then provides minimal security assurance. Check the audit date and the protocol's update history.
  • Vague firm credentials — If you've never heard of the audit firm, search for them. Look for their past reports, whether they've found real vulnerabilities in other protocols, and whether they have genuine Solana expertise. Unknown firms with polished websites but no track record are a red flag.

⚠️ The "Audit Theater" Trap: Some protocols hire low-quality audit firms, receive a report with zero findings (which is itself suspicious — no code is perfect), and use that report as a marketing badge. A clean audit with zero Critical, High, Medium, or Low findings is often more suspicious than one with a reasonable number of identified and fixed issues. Genuine auditors find real bugs. If the report seems too clean, read it more carefully.

Audit Completeness Checklist: What to Verify Before Depositing

Use this checklist to systematically evaluate any Solana DeFi protocol's security posture before depositing funds. Each item represents a layer of defense — missing multiple layers is a significant risk signal.

  • Audit date is recent — The audit should be from within the last 6-12 months. If the protocol has had major upgrades since the audit, the audit is effectively stale.
  • Audited by a top-tier firm — At minimum, one of: Neodyme, OtterSec, Kudelski, Halborn, Trail of Bits, or another firm with a proven Solana track record. Two independent audits are better than one.
  • All Critical and High findings are resolved — The report should show "Fixed" or "Resolved" status for all Critical and High findings. "Acknowledged" for High-severity issues requires careful evaluation of the risk.
  • Remediated code is verifiable — The protocol or audit report links to specific commit hashes showing the fixes. You should be able to confirm the remediated code is what's deployed.
  • Scope covers the deposit interface — The audit must cover the specific program(s) that handle user deposits, withdrawals, and balance accounting. An audit that only covers a peripheral contract (e.g., governance) is insufficient.
  • Economic/mathematical review was performed — For DeFi protocols, the audit should include economic modeling to check for oracle manipulation, price calculation exploits, and liquidation logic.
  • Protocol has a bug bounty program — A live bug bounty (via Immunefi, HackenProof, or similar) indicates ongoing security commitment beyond the initial audit. Bounties should cover at least 10% of total value locked (TVL) for Critical findings.
  • Admin keys are secured — The protocol should use multisig wallets (ideally Squads or a similar Solana-native multisig) with timelocks for any admin functions. Single-key admin control is a major risk regardless of audit quality.
  • Upgrade authority is restricted — On Solana, many DeFi protocols use upgradable programs via the BPF upgrade mechanism. Check if the upgrade authority is a multisig and whether there's a timelock on upgrades. No timelock means the team can change the code at any time.
  • Ongoing monitoring is in place — The protocol should have real-time monitoring for suspicious activity (via Sentinel, Chaos Labs, or custom tooling). Some protocols publish a security dashboard showing live system health.
  • Insurance or coverage fund exists — Some Solana DeFi protocols participate in insurance protocols (like Insurance on Solana) or maintain a coverage fund for unforeseen exploits. This doesn't replace security but adds a layer of user protection.
  • Protocol has been live without major incidents — Time since launch without a critical exploit is a practical (though not foolproof) indicator of security. Protocols that have operated for 6+ months without incident have survived more real-world testing.

🔑 The 3-Minute Audit Check: When evaluating a protocol, open the audit report and look for three things immediately: (1) the audit date and scope section — what exactly was reviewed and when; (2) the findings summary table — how many Critical, High, Medium, Low findings were found and what their status is; (3) the "Acknowledged" risks — what issues did the team accept and why. If any of these three sections raise concerns, dig deeper before depositing.

Protocols Without Audits: When You're the Unpaid QA Team

As of early 2026, thousands of Solana DeFi protocols exist, and a significant percentage — particularly smaller platforms, new launches, and yield-bearing token projects — have no public audit at all. Some never will.

Depositing funds into an unaudited Solana protocol is a fundamentally different risk category. Here's what you need to understand:

Why Some Protocols Don't Have Audits

  • Cost barrier — A full Solana audit from a top firm costs $50,000 to $200,000+. Smaller teams often can't afford it.
  • Speed to market — Audits take 4-12 weeks. In the fast-moving Solana ecosystem, some teams launch first and plan to audit later.
  • Intentionally evading scrutiny — Protocols designed to rug or exploit users deliberately skip audits because an audit would identify their malicious code.
  • Misunderstanding of value — Some teams genuinely don't understand that an audit is table stakes for DeFi in 2026.

How to Evaluate an Unaudited Protocol

If you choose to engage with unaudited protocols (a higher-risk strategy), apply additional scrutiny:

  • Read the source code yourself — If the protocol is open source (most legitimate Solana protocols verify their code on Solscan or GitHub), review the deposit and withdrawal functions at minimum. Look for obvious red flags like unlimited mint functions, hardcoded addresses, or owner-only withdrawal functions.
  • Check for a public bug bounty — Some protocols skip a full audit but run a bug bounty program. This isn't equivalent to an audit (bounties are reactive, audits are proactive), but it's better than nothing.
  • Examine the team — Doxxed teams with proven track records in the Solana ecosystem are less likely to rug than anonymous teams with no history. Check their GitHub activity, previous projects, and community reputation.
  • Start small — Never deposit your full intended amount into an unaudited protocol at once. Test with a minimal deposit first, monitor the behavior, and scale up only after you're comfortable.
  • Monitor community discussion — Check the protocol's Discord and Telegram for security-related discussions. Community members often surface issues before they become critical — but also watch for coordinated shilling that drowns out legitimate concerns.

⚠️ Bottom line: In 2026, an unaudited Solana DeFi protocol is not suitable for any funds you can't afford to lose entirely. The absence of an audit does not guarantee a rug, but it is the single strongest predictor of smart contract risk. Treat unaudited protocols as experimental — they may offer higher yields precisely because those yields come with uncompensated security risk.

Ongoing Monitoring: Why One Audit Is Never Enough

The most sophisticated Solana DeFi protocols don't treat security as a one-time event. They implement continuous monitoring and regular re-audits as part of their operational cycle. Here's what ongoing security looks like in practice:

Real-Time Threat Monitoring

Platforms like Sentinel (built on Solana), Chaos Labs, and OpenZeppelin Defender provide real-time monitoring for suspicious on-chain activity. These systems track:

  • Unusual large withdrawals or transfers from protocol wallets
  • Suspicious oracle price movements that could indicate manipulation
  • Rapid changes in TVL that might signal an exploit in progress
  • Unexpected program interactions or upgrade attempts

Regular Re-Audits and Updates

Best practice among top-tier Solana DeFi protocols is to commission a fresh audit every 6-12 months or after any significant upgrade. Some protocols now publish a "security update log" showing each audit date, scope change, and remediation status over time. This transparency is a strong positive signal.

Security Dashboards and Transparency Reports

A growing number of Solana DeFi protocols publish live security dashboards that include:

  • Current multisig configuration and signer status
  • Timelock status for pending upgrades
  • Current bug bounty amounts and payout history
  • Links to all audit reports with version tracking
  • Incident response runbooks and contact information

Protocols that publish these dashboards are signaling that security is a priority, not a checkbox. Protocols that have outdated audit links, broken report PDFs, or no security information at all are signaling the opposite — regardless of what their marketing says.

The Solana DeFi Security Maturity Model

When evaluating a protocol's overall security posture, think in terms of maturity levels:

  • Level 1 (Basic): Has a single audit, possibly outdated. No bug bounty. Admin keys are single-signature. No monitoring.
  • Level 2 (Standard): Recent audit from a reputable firm. Bug bounty live on Immunefi. Admin keys use multisig. Some monitoring in place.
  • Level 3 (Advanced): Multiple independent audits. Re-audits every 6 months. Timelocked upgrades. Real-time monitoring with automated alerts. Published security dashboard.
  • Level 4 (Elite): All of Level 3 plus formal verification of critical components. Insurance coverage. Independent security committee with on-chain veto power. Transparent incident response drills.

For most retail depositors, protocols at Level 2 or above represent a reasonable risk-reward profile for DeFi yield strategies. Level 1 protocols require careful scrutiny and smaller position sizes. Unaudited protocols (Level 0) should be treated as experimental gambling, not investment.

Putting It All Together: Your Pre-Deposit Audit Workflow

Before depositing into any Solana DeFi protocol, run through this 5-step workflow. It takes 10-15 minutes and can save you from losing your entire deposit:

  1. Find the audit reports. Check the protocol website, docs, GitHub repository, and social media announcements. If you can't find the audit reports easily, that's a red flag. Legitimate protocols prominently link to their audits.
  2. Verify the audit firm. Confirm the audit was conducted by a firm with a verifiable Solana track record. Cross-reference the report on the firm's own website or GitHub — don't rely solely on the protocol's hosted PDF, which could be a forgery.
  3. Read the findings summary. Look at the severity table. Note the number of Critical, High, Medium findings and their statuses (Fixed, Acknowledged, Partially Fixed). Pay special attention to any unresolved High-severity issues.
  4. Check the scope and date. Verify that the audit covers the specific program you'll be depositing into. Check that the audit is recent enough to cover the current codebase. If the protocol has upgraded since the audit, the protections are reduced.
  5. Verify ongoing security. Check for a live bug bounty, multisig configuration, upgrade timelocks, and any monitoring infrastructure. A protocol that passed an audit six months ago but has had three upgrades and no re-audit since is less secure than its audit suggests.

📋 Pro tip: Bookmark the audit report PDF and the specific commit hash of the audited code. If a protocol ever gets exploited, you can check whether the vulnerability existed in the audited code or was introduced in a post-audit upgrade. This information is invaluable for post-mortem analysis and legal recourse if applicable.

FAQ: Solana DeFi Audit Quick Answers

How much does a Solana smart contract audit cost?

Top-tier Solana audits typically range from $50,000 to $200,000+ depending on code complexity, scope, and the firm's reputation. Neodyme and OtterSec are generally in the $75,000-$150,000 range for a standard DeFi protocol.

How long does a Solana audit take?

A thorough audit takes 4-12 weeks including initial review, remediation period, and re-review. Simple protocols might be done in 3-4 weeks; complex lending or derivatives platforms can take 8-12 weeks.

Can an audited protocol still get hacked?

Yes. Audits reduce risk but don't eliminate it entirely. Hacks can occur through post-audit upgrades, undiscovered vulnerabilities, economic exploits that auditors didn't model correctly, or non-smart-contract attack vectors (front-end compromise, DNS hijacking, private key theft).

What's the difference between an audit and a bug bounty?

An audit is a proactive, time-boxed review by security professionals. A bug bounty is a continuous, incentivized program where the broader security community can report vulnerabilities. They complement each other — a protocol should ideally have both.

How many audits should a protocol have?

Two independent audits from reputable firms is the industry best practice. Some top Solana protocols (Jupiter, Marinade, Solend) have 3-4 audits plus continuous bug bounties. One audit is the minimum acceptable standard for depositing any significant amount.

Are small audit firms safe?

Not necessarily. The audit industry is unregulated, and anyone can call themselves a smart contract auditor. Stick with firms that have verifiable Solana track records, published reports you can read, and a history of finding real vulnerabilities in known protocols.
