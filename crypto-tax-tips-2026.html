Crypto Tax Tips for 2026: What Every Investor Must Know

Crypto tax season is more complex than ever in 2026. With the IRS increasing enforcement and new reporting requirements from exchanges, staying compliant is critical. Here is what every crypto investor needs to know.

Every Trade Is a Taxable Event

When you sell crypto for fiat, trade one crypto for another, use crypto to buy goods or services, or earn crypto through staking or rewards — each of these is a taxable event. You must report the fair market value at the time of the transaction and calculate your capital gain or loss. The IRS treats crypto as property, not currency, for tax purposes.

Short-Term vs Long-Term Gains

If you hold crypto for one year or less before selling, gains are taxed as short-term at your ordinary income tax rate (up to 37%). Hold for more than one year and gains are taxed as long-term at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your income bracket). Tax-loss harvesting — selling losing positions to offset gains — is a powerful strategy to reduce your tax bill.

Tracking Tools

Manual tracking is nearly impossible if you trade frequently. Use crypto tax software like CoinTracker, Koinly, or TaxBit. These tools connect to your exchanges and wallets via API, automatically calculate gains and losses, and generate the IRS forms you need (8949, Schedule D). Many exchanges now issue 1099-DA forms, making reconciliation easier.

Staking and DeFi Income

Staking rewards, DeFi yields, and airdrops are taxed as ordinary income at their fair market value when received. If you later sell these tokens, any gain or loss from the income price is a capital gain or loss. Keep detailed records of when you received each reward and its value at that time. This is the area most likely to trigger an IRS audit.
