How Pump.fun Actually Works

Pump.fun is, at its core, a fair-launch token creation platform built on Solana. It solves two fundamental problems that previously prevented casual users from creating tokens: the cost of establishing liquidity and the complexity of smart contract deployment.

The Core Innovation

Traditional DEX launches require a creator to provide initial liquidity ΓÇö typically depositing an equal value of SOL and the new token into a liquidity pool on Raydium or Orca. This upfront capital requirement (often $500ΓÇô$5,000+) was the primary barrier. Pump.fun eliminates this by using an internal bonding curve that creates a built-in market for the token from the moment it exists. No external liquidity pool is needed until the token reaches a predetermined market cap threshold.

Key Features

  • Zero upfront liquidity: The bonding curve acts as an automated market maker from token zero.
  • Fixed supply: Each token has a capped total supply (typically 1 billion tokens).
  • No presale or team allocation: All tokens are released through the bonding curve ΓÇö there is no hidden team supply.
  • Built-in buy/sell mechanics: Users buy from and sell to the bonding curve, not to other users.
  • Developer fee: A small fee on each transaction funds the platform.
  • Graduation mechanism: When the market cap hits the threshold (~$69,000 SOL equivalent), the bonding curve is disabled and liquidity migrates to Raydium.

The brilliance of this design is its simplicity. By baking the trading mechanism directly into the token's creation, Pump.fun removes the need for external pool creation, liquidity provider tokens, or any of the DeFi infrastructure that traditionally made token launching a developer-only activity.

Note: While "token creation platform" sounds like these are smart contract factories, Pump.fun does not deploy unique contracts for each token. All tokens use a standard Pump.fun program, and the platform maintains the bonding curve state. This means tokens cannot be modified, renounced, or upgraded ΓÇö what you see at launch is what you get.

The Bonding Curve Model Explained

The bonding curve is the mathematical engine that powers every token on Pump.fun. Understanding it is essential to understanding why tokens behave the way they do on these platforms.

What Is a Bonding Curve?

A bonding curve is a mathematical formula that defines the relationship between a token's supply and its price. As more tokens are purchased (supply decreases), the price increases along the curve. As tokens are sold (supply increases), the price decreases. The curve is deterministic ΓÇö at any given supply level, the price is precisely calculable.

Pump.fun's Specific Curve

Pump.fun uses a linear bonding curve with a fixed slope. The starting price is extremely low ΓÇö fractions of a cent ΓÇö and the ending price at full supply is the threshold that triggers graduation. The curve is designed so that the first buyers get in at the lowest prices, creating an incentive for early participation.

Here is how the math works in practice:

  • Starting price: Approximately $0.00000002 per token (at 1 billion total supply, this means the entire market cap is only a few dollars at launch).
  • Mid-curve price: By the time 50% of the supply has been bought, the price has increased roughly 10ΓÇô20x from the starting price.
  • Graduation threshold: At ~$69,000 market cap (approximately 85-90% of tokens sold from the curve), the bonding curve closes and the remaining liquidity migrates to Raydium.

Why Bonding Curves Matter for Traders

The bonding curve creates several important dynamics:

  • Price impact is predictable: You can calculate exactly how much a buy or sell will move the price based on the curve gradient.
  • Early buyers have structural advantage: The curve rewards early entry with lower prices, but also means late buyers face steeper costs.
  • Sell pressure compresses prices: When early buyers sell, the price drops along the curve ΓÇö often rapidly, since the curve is steepest near the end.
  • Graduation resets the market: Once on Raydium, the price is determined by the AMM pool, not the curve ΓÇö a completely different dynamic.

ΓÜá Important: The bonding curve is a closed system. When you buy on Pump.fun, you are buying from the curve ΓÇö not from other holders. This means there is always liquidity to sell (unlike some DEX pairs), but the amount you receive depends entirely on where you are on the curve when you exit.

Graduation to Raydium: What Happens When a Coin "Makes It"

The graduation event is the most important milestone in a Pump.fun token's lifecycle. When the bonding curve reaches its terminal market cap (~$69,000), the platform automatically executes a migration to Raydium, one of Solana's largest decentralized exchanges.

The Graduation Process

  1. Bonding curve completion: The curve reaches ~85-90% of total supply sold. The remaining tokens (~10-15% of supply) are reserved for the initial Raydium pool.
  2. Automated liquidity deployment: The platform automatically creates a Raydium liquidity pool using the remaining tokens from the curve plus an equivalent value of SOL collected from trading fees.
  3. Initial pool price: The opening price on Raydium is set based on the last bonding curve price, ensuring continuity.
  4. Pump.fun trading stops: Once graduated, the token can no longer be traded on Pump.fun. All trading moves to Raydium.
  5. Liquidity is locked: The Raydium liquidity pool tokens (LP tokens) are burned or locked, meaning the liquidity cannot be removed by the creator.

What Graduation Means for Price Action

Graduation is a double-edged event. On one hand, it signals that a token had enough organic demand to fill an entire bonding curve ΓÇö a genuine achievement when 99% of tokens never reach this stage. On the other hand, graduation opens the token to a much larger market with different dynamics:

  • Raydium bots and arbitrageurs immediately start trading, often causing massive volatility in the first minutes.
  • Early bonding curve holders who bought near the start now have 100x+ paper gains and may sell immediately.
  • The pool is relatively small (~$69,000 initial liquidity), meaning a single large whale can move the price dramatically.
  • Visibility increases as the token appears on Raydium listings, DEX aggregators, and tracking sites.

Stat: Fewer than 1% of all tokens created on Pump.fun ever graduate to Raydium. Of those that do graduate, approximately 30-40% experience a significant price decline within the first 24 hours on Raydium as early holders take profits. The real test of a token begins after graduation.

Once confirmed, your token appears on Pump.fun's trading interface. Other users can immediately buy and sell it through the bonding curve. The token is also indexed by Solana block explorers like Solscan within seconds.

Risks of New Tokens: Rug Pulls, Honeypots, and Scams

While Pump.fun's design eliminates some traditional risks (no team token allocation, locked liquidity on graduation), it does not eliminate all risk. In fact, the sheer volume of tokens creates a different risk landscape altogether.

Category 1: Structural Risks Inherent to the Platform

  • Extreme volatility: Early-stage bonding curve tokens can swing 50-90% in minutes. The thin liquidity at the start means small buys cause massive price jumps, and small sells cause equally massive crashes.
  • Information asymmetry: Token creators have full control over social narratives, timing, and marketing. They know exactly when they will buy or sell their own token, while retail traders react after the fact.
  • No fundamental value: Almost all tokens on these platforms have zero intrinsic value, revenue, or utility. Their price is entirely driven by speculation and narrative momentum.

Category 2: Developer-Driven Risks

  • Developer sells at launch: While the bonding curve prevents massive dumps, creators can still buy early (before listing) and sell immediately, taking profits at the expense of later buyers.
  • Multi-wallet manipulation: Creators can deploy multiple wallets to create artificial buy pressure, making the token look more popular than it is.
  • Abandoned tokens: The vast majority of tokens receive zero promotion and are abandoned within hours. Holders are left with tokens that have no community, no trading volume, and no path to graduation.

Category 3: Post-Graduation Risks

  • Raydium dump: The most common post-graduation pattern is a massive sell-off by early bonding curve buyers who now have liquidity on Raydium.
  • No organic demand: Even if a token graduates, there may be no real community or demand ΓÇö just speculators flipping the bonding curve.
  • LP manipulation: While Pump.fun locks the initial liquidity, creators or early holders can still manipulate the Raydium pool through large trades.

Category 4: Platform-Specific Scams

Some token creation platforms have fewer safeguards than Pump.fun. Be wary of:

  • Honeypot tokens: Tokens that allow buys but block sells through custom smart contract logic.
  • Hidden mint functions: Tokens where the creator retains the ability to mint unlimited new supply.
  • Fake graduated tokens: Tokens that appear to have graduated to Raydium but actually use a clone pool with no locked liquidity.

ΓÜá Critical: According to on-chain analysis from 2025-2026, approximately 98% of tokens created on Pump.fun lose 90%+ of their value within the first 24 hours. Less than 0.5% of tokens ever graduate to Raydium. Never invest more than you are willing to lose completely. These are not investments ΓÇö they are speculative bets.
