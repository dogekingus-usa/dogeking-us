Solana NFT Royalties: How Creator Royalties Work on Solana and Why They Matter for NFT Investors

In 2022, the Solana NFT ecosystem erupted in controversy. Tens of thousands of traders, collectors, and creators clashed over a single question: should NFT creators be paid royalties on secondary sales?

What followed — the "Royalty Wars" — reshaped the entire Solana NFT landscape. Marketplaces split into opposing camps. Some of the largest NFT collections threatened to leave platforms that made royalties optional. Creators watched their revenue streams evaporate overnight. By 2026, the dust has partially settled. Royalty enforcement on Solana has evolved through a combination of on-chain technical standards, marketplace policies, and community norms. But the debate is far from over. How Royalty Mechanics Work on Solana

Before diving into the controversy, you need to understand the technical foundation. NFT royalties on Solana are fundamentally different from royalties on Ethereum — and that difference is at the core of everything that followed.

On Ethereum, the ERC-721 and ERC-1155 token standards do not include native royalty enforcement. Instead, royalties are handled through an external standard called EIP-2981 (the NFT Royalty Standard), which is a "callback" mechanism: marketplaces can choose to honor or ignore the royalty information stored in the smart contract. There is no on-chain enforcement. If a marketplace chooses not to pay royalties, the creator has no way to block the transfer.

Solana took a different path. The Metaplex Token Metadata standard — which powers the vast majority of Solana NFT collections, including giants like DeGods, y00ts, Okay Bears, and Tensorians — embeds royalty information directly into each NFT's on-chain metadata account. The Seller Fee Basis Points field (measured in basis points, where 500 = 5%) is written into the metadata at mint time. The Creators field stores up to five wallet addresses along with their share percentages and a verified flag.

This on-chain metadata is what marketplaces read to determine who should receive royalties and how much. But — and this is the critical point — the Solana protocol itself does not enforce royalty payments. The SPL Token program has no built-in royalty logic. Every marketplace, aggregator, and peer-to-peer trading protocol chooses whether to honor the metadata's royalty instructions.

Key Technical Detail: Solana NFT metadata is stored in a Program Derived Address (PDA) account owned by the Metaplex Token Metadata program. This means the metadata is not stored on the token itself (which is just an SPL Token mint) but in a separate account linked to the token. When an NFT is transferred, the metadata account stays with the mint, so royalty information persists across ownership changes. The metadata can also be updated — which became a weapon in the royalty wars.

The Metaplex Royalty Standard: Seller Fee Basis Points

The core royalty mechanism in Metaplex's Token Metadata standard works as follows:

  • Seller Fee Basis Points (SFBP): A single number representing the royalty percentage, stored as basis points. 500 = 5%, 1000 = 10%, 0 = no royalties. Most Solana NFT collections set this between 500 and 1000 (5%–10%).
  • Creators Array: An array of up to five creator wallet addresses, each with an individual share (percentage of the total royalty) and a verified boolean flag. Verified creators have signed the metadata, proving they are the original creator.
  • Royalty Payouts: When a sale occurs, the marketplace calculates the total royalty amount (sale price × SFBP / 10000) and distributes it among the creators according to their share percentages. The remaining amount goes to the seller.

This system works well when marketplaces choose to honor it. The problem? Throughout 2022 and 2023, an increasing number of marketplaces chose not to.

Enforced vs Optional Royalties: The Solana Divide

The fundamental fault line in the Solana NFT royalty debate is the difference between enforced royalties and optional royalties. Understanding this distinction is critical for every NFT investor because it directly affects the price you pay, the liquidity you receive, and the long-term health of the collections you invest in.

Enforced Royalties (On-Chain Enforcement)

Enforced royalties use on-chain mechanisms to make royalty payments mandatory. On Solana, the most significant enforcement mechanism is the Metaplex Royalty Enforcement system, introduced in late 2024 as part of the Metaplex Core standard. This standard adds a royalty enforcement flag to the NFT metadata that marketplaces and programs can read programmatically. If a marketplace attempts to transfer an NFT without paying the stated royalties, the token's metadata account can reject the transfer at the program level.

How enforcement works technically: Metaplex Core NFTs carry an immutable Royalty Enforcement field backed by a rule set — a collection of programmable constraints that govern how the NFT can be transferred. The rule set can check that a royalty payment was included in the transaction before allowing the transfer to complete. If the royalty is missing or insufficient, the rule set blocks the transfer entirely.

Investor Impact: NFTs with enforced royalties always cost more to acquire — you must pay the full royalty on every purchase. But enforced royalties signal a committed creator team with a sustainable business model. Collections with enforced royalties tend to have stronger community engagement, more active development, and better post-mint support. For long-term investors, enforced royalties are generally a positive signal.

Optional Royalties (Marketplace Discretion)

Optional royalties mean the marketplace or trading protocol chooses whether to enforce payment. On Solana, most optional-royalty models work on a "suggested" or "honor system" basis. The marketplace reads the SFBP from the metadata and presents the buyer with a recommended royalty amount — but the buyer can choose to pay less or nothing at all.

Some marketplaces use dynamic royalty minimums: for example, a marketplace might enforce 100% of royalties for the first 30 days after mint (to support initial creator revenue) and then reduce enforcement to 50% or 0% after that period. Others use tiered systems where holders with higher wallet balances or trading volumes pay reduced royalties.

Key Warning for Investors: If you buy an NFT from a collection that relies on optional royalties, check the collection's recent trading volume. Collections where most trading happens on optional-royalty platforms often see creator teams abandon development after mint because their ongoing revenue stream dried up. This can destroy the long-term value of your NFT.

How Tensor and Magic Eden Handle Royalties in 2026

Two marketplaces dominate Solana NFT trading in 2026 — Tensor and Magic Eden — and they have taken fundamentally different approaches to royalties. Understanding these policies is essential because the marketplace you choose to trade on directly affects the royalties you pay or receive.

Tensor: Pro-Trader, Minimal Royalties

Tensor emerged as the dominant Solana NFT marketplace by aggressively prioritizing trader experience over creator royalties. Tensor's core philosophy is that NFTs should trade like any other asset — with minimal friction and maximum liquidity.

Tensor's royalty policy in 2026:

  • Default: 0% enforced royalties on all collections unless the collection has opted into Metaplex's on-chain royalty enforcement (Metaplex Core with binding rule sets).
  • Dynamic Royalty Toggle: Buyers can choose to add a "creator tip" of 1%–10% on any purchase. This is entirely voluntary. Less than 8% of Tensor users opt in.
  • Tensorian and Partner Collections: Collections with strong Tensor relationships — like Tensorians themselves, and select high-volume collections — may receive "featured" status that encourages but does not require royalty payments.
  • Zero Royalty on Bids: When you place a bid on Tensor (which is how most NFT trading happens on the platform), the bid price is what the seller receives. Royalties are not deducted from bids. If the bid is accepted, the buyer pays the bid price plus any marketplace fees — no mandatory creator royalty.

Tensor's approach has been wildly successful for trading volume. As of early 2026, Tensor accounts for roughly 65% of all Solana NFT trading volume. The platform's zero-royalty default has attracted high-frequency traders, flippers, and arbitrageurs who would otherwise trade on Ethereum or other chains.

Investor Takeaway: If you are a short-term trader flipping NFTs for quick profits, Tensor is the most cost-effective marketplace because you avoid royalty costs on every trade. For buy-and-hold investors, however, the ubiquity of zero-royalty trading on Tensor means your NFT's resale value may be lower because future buyers will factor in that they can sell without paying royalties.

Magic Eden: Pro-Creator, Flexible Enforcement

Magic Eden — the original dominant Solana NFT marketplace — took a sharp turn toward creator protection after the royalty wars. In 2026, Magic Eden positions itself as the pro-creator marketplace.

Magic Eden's royalty policy in 2026:

  • Full Royalty Enforcement for Opted-In Collections: Collections that use Metaplex's on-chain royalty enforcement (or Magic Eden's own Royalty Shield program) have royalties enforced at the marketplace level. Magic Eden will not list an NFT for sale at a price that does not cover the full royalty.
  • Royalty Shield: Magic Eden's proprietary royalty enforcement system that checks the collection's royalty configuration at the time of listing. If the collection has a verified creator and a non-zero SFBP, Magic Eden enforces the full royalty. Royalty Shield covers over 4,000 Solana NFT collections as of May 2026.
  • Dual Listing: Collections can choose to list on Magic Eden with enforced royalties while the same NFTs trade on Tensor with zero royalties. This creates an interesting dynamic — sellers who want to enforce royalties list on Magic Eden; buyers who want to avoid them buy on Tensor.
  • Creator Dashboard: Magic Eden provides a creator dashboard showing real-time royalty accruals, payment history, and analytics. This has made Magic Eden the preferred platform for professional NFT studios and artists.

Magic Eden's market share is roughly 25% of Solana NFT volume — down from over 90% in 2022 — but it remains the dominant platform for premium collections and creator-launched projects.

The Royalty Wars: A Brief History of Solana NFT's Biggest Controversy

To understand the current state of Solana NFT royalties, you need to understand the conflict that shaped it. The "Royalty Wars" of 2022–2024 were a series of marketplace decisions, community battles, and technical innovations that permanently fractured the Solana NFT ecosystem.

Phase 1: The Golden Era (2021–Mid 2022)

In Solana NFT's early days, every major marketplace — Magic Eden, Solanart, DigitalEyes, and Alpha Art — enforced creator royalties by default. The standard was 5–10% on secondary sales. Creators built business models around these royalties, hiring teams, funding marketing campaigns, and planning long-term roadmaps. Royalties were simply part of the NFT social contract.

Phase 2: The First Cracks (Late 2022)

The bear market of 2022 changed everything. As NFT prices collapsed and trading volumes plummeted, traders began demanding lower fees. New marketplaces — led by Yawww and later Tensor — launched with zero-royalty models, arguing that mandatory royalties hurt liquidity and pushed traders to Ethereum. Yawww's launch was the opening shot. Within weeks, trading volume shifted dramatically toward zero-royalty platforms.

Magic Eden initially defended royalties, but by October 2022, the pressure was too great. Magic Eden announced it was moving to optional royalties, allowing buyers to choose whether to pay. The creator community erupted. Major collections including DeGods, Okay Bears, and Aurory threatened to fork Magic Eden or leave the platform entirely.

Phase 3: The Fork Threats and Creator Revolt (2023)

In response to Magic Eden's optional royalty decision, several major Solana NFT projects explored building their own marketplaces or forking existing infrastructure. The most significant move came from DeGods and y00ts creator Frank DeGods, who launched the Dust Protocol — a royalty enforcement mechanism that burned NFTs traded on non-compliant marketplaces. This was a nuclear option: trade on a zero-royalty platform, and your NFT gets destroyed.

Dust Protocol sparked massive debate. Proponents argued it gave creators the power to enforce their business model. Critics argued it violated the principle of NFT ownership — if you own an NFT, you should be able to trade it anywhere.

Meanwhile, the technical arms race accelerated. Metaplex — the team behind the Solana NFT standard — began working on on-chain royalty enforcement at the protocol level. Their goal: make royalty enforcement a property of the NFT itself, not a choice of the marketplace.

Timeline Highlight — November 2023: Tensor surpassed Magic Eden in monthly Solana NFT trading volume for the first time. The shift to zero-royalty trading was confirmed as the dominant market trend. Analysts estimated that Solana NFT creators collectively lost over $200 million in potential royalty revenue during 2023 alone.

Phase 4: Metaplex Core and On-Chain Enforcement (2024)

The game-changer arrived in mid-2024 when Metaplex launched Metaplex Core, a new NFT standard that included native royalty enforcement as a core feature. Metaplex Core introduced the Royalty Enforcement field backed by rule sets — programmable constraints that govern how NFTs are transferred.

Under Metaplex Core, creators can choose one of three royalty enforcement levels:

  • None: No enforcement. Marketplaces can choose to honor or ignore royalties.
  • Soft Enforcement: The NFT metadata requests royalties, and marketplaces that respect the request show a "Royalty Compliant" badge. Non-compliant marketplaces are flagged.
  • Hard Enforcement: The NFT's rule set blocks any transfer that does not include the full royalty payment. This is true on-chain enforcement — no marketplace can bypass it without forking the Solana program.

Metaplex Core adoption grew rapidly through 2025. By May 2026, over 85% of new Solana NFT collections are minted using Metaplex Core with at least soft enforcement. Older collections minted under the legacy Metaplex standard have the option to migrate, but migration is voluntary and requires community consensus.

How Royalties Affect NFT Pricing and Liquidity

For NFT investors, the royalty debate is not theoretical. Royalties directly impact the two most important investment metrics: price and liquidity.

The Pricing Impact: Royalties as a Hidden Cost

Every time an NFT changes hands on a royalty-enforcing platform, a 5–10% fee is deducted from the sale price and paid to the creator. This has a compound effect on pricing over multiple trades.

Consider a hypothetical NFT with a 10% royalty that trades four times over two years:

  • Initial buyer: Pays $1,000 for the NFT at mint. No royalty is paid (primary sale).
  • First secondary sale ($1,500): Seller receives $1,350. Creator receives $150. The buyer pays $1,500.
  • Second secondary sale ($2,000): Seller receives $1,800. Creator receives $200. The buyer pays $2,000.
  • Third secondary sale ($2,500): Seller receives $2,250. Creator receives $250. The buyer pays $2,500.
  • Fourth secondary sale ($3,000): Seller receives $2,700. Creator receives $300. The buyer pays $3,000.

Total royalties paid over four trades: $900 — effectively, 10% of the total transaction value was extracted from traders and given to the creator. Now compare this to a zero-royalty scenario on Tensor: the seller receives the full sale price minus marketplace fees (typically 1–2%). The creator receives nothing.

Liquidity Trap: High royalties can create a price floor gap — the minimum price a seller is willing to accept (after accounting for royalties they must pay) versus the maximum price a buyer is willing to offer. When this gap widens, trading volume drops, and NFTs become "sticky" — listed but rarely traded. Collections with 10% enforced royalties on Magic Eden often show 20–30% less trading volume than identical collections on Tensor.

Liquidity: The Royalty-Liquidity Tradeoff

The relationship between royalties and liquidity is well-documented in Solana NFT data:

  • Zero-royalty platforms (Tensor): Higher trading velocity, tighter bid-ask spreads, more active listings, and faster price discovery. The average time-to-sale for an NFT on Tensor is roughly half that of Magic Eden for the same collection tier.
  • Full-royalty platforms (Magic Eden, Exchange.Art): Lower trading velocity, wider spreads, but higher per-trade value retention for creators. Asks tend to be higher because sellers factor in the royalty deduction.

This creates a market bifurcation: serious collectors and long-term holders gravitate toward Magic Eden (where they pay more but support creators), while short-term traders and flippers cluster on Tensor (where they minimize costs). The same NFT can trade at different effective prices on different platforms — a phenomenon known as royalty-driven arbitrage.

Investor Strategy: If you are buying an NFT you plan to hold long-term (6+ months), consider paying the full royalty on Magic Eden. You are investing in the creator's continued development, which supports the floor price. If you are flipping an NFT within days or weeks, trade on Tensor where royalties are minimal. Just be aware that every trade on Tensor that skips royalties reduces the creator's incentive to support the project.

Creator Perspectives: Why Royalties Matter Beyond Revenue

For NFT creators, royalties are not just about money — they represent a fundamental shift in how digital artists can build sustainable careers.

In traditional art markets, an artist sells a work once. Unless they have a contract guaranteeing future sale splits (which is rare and difficult to enforce), they do not benefit if their work appreciates in value. A digital artist who sold an NFT for 1 SOL in 2021 might watch that same NFT trade for 1,000 SOL two years later — and receive nothing from the windfall.

NFT royalties were supposed to fix this. By embedding a 5–10% royalty into the token's metadata, creators would participate in the upside of their work, earning passive revenue every time their art changed hands. For the first time, digital artists could build recurring revenue streams from secondary sales — the same way musicians earn streaming royalties or authors earn book royalties.

The optional royalty movement broke this promise. As one prominent Solana artist told us: "I spent six months building my collection, paying for marketing, managing community, and delivering on a roadmap. The day Tensor made royalties optional, my projected lifetime earnings dropped by 70%. That's not a marketplace innovation — that's taking money out of creators' pockets and calling it efficiency."

The Creator Business Model in 2026

In response to the royalty wars, successful Solana NFT creators have adapted their business models:

  • Upfront Mint Revenue: Creators now raise larger amounts during the initial mint to compensate for uncertain secondary revenue. Mint prices have increased — from typical 0.5–1 SOL in 2022 to 1–3 SOL for premium collections in 2026.
  • Royalty Enforcement as a Premium Feature: Collections that enforce royalties position themselves as premium investments. The enforced royalty signals that the creator is serious about long-term support, which justifies a higher floor price.
  • Dual Revenue Streams: Many creators now combine mint revenue with utility revenue — charging subscription fees for Discord access, paid events, or exclusive token-gated content. This reduces dependence on secondary royalties.
  • Marketplace Alliances: Creators negotiate directly with marketplaces. A collection launching on Magic Eden might receive guaranteed royalty enforcement for 12 months in exchange for an exclusive launch window.

How Royalties Affect Different NFT Investment Strategies

Not all NFTs are affected by royalties equally. Understanding how royalties impact different NFT categories helps you choose the right investment approach.

PFP Collections (Profile Pictures)

Blue-chip PFPs like DeGods, Tensorians, and Mad Lads typically have enforced royalties (5–7%). These collections have strong brands, active communities, and ongoing development. The royalty enforcement is part of the value proposition — it signals quality and commitment. For investors, paying the royalty on these collections is usually justified because the brand strength supports the floor price.

Generative Art and 1/1s

Generative art collections on platforms like Exchange.Art and fx(hash) typically have higher royalties (7.5–10%). These are often individual artists rather than studios, and royalties are a primary income source. Paying the full royalty here directly supports the artist's ability to keep creating. Liquidity is naturally lower for 1/1 artworks, so the royalty-liquidity tradeoff is less of an issue.

Gaming and Utility NFTs

Solana gaming NFTs (think Star Atlas ships or Aurory assets) often have lower or zero royalties. The business model is usually transaction-based — the game takes fees from in-game actions rather than NFT trades. Royalties matter less here because the value is in the game's utility, not the secondary trading market.

Meme Coins as NFTs

The blurring line between NFTs and meme coins on Solana has created a new category. Some meme coin projects issue tokenized NFTs (like compressed NFTs representing governance rights or airdrop eligibility) with near-zero royalties. These are pure trading assets where royalty costs would be value-destructive.

The Future of Solana NFT Royalties: Where We Are Heading

The Solana NFT royalty landscape in 2026 is a fragile equilibrium — but the battle is not over. Several trends will shape the next phase.

1. Metaplex Core Becomes the Default Standard

By late 2026, Metaplex Core is expected to become the default NFT standard for all new Solana NFT mints. Legacy Metaplex (Token Metadata) will gradually be phased out. This means hard royalty enforcement will become the default for new collections. The question is how many existing collections will migrate. Expect major blue-chip collections to migrate by end of 2026, while smaller collections may stay on the legacy standard due to migration costs.

2. Marketplace Consolidation Around Two Tiers

The Solana NFT marketplace landscape is converging toward two tiers: Tensor for volume trading (low fees, minimal royalties, high speed) and Magic Eden for premium collecting (full royalties, creator tools, curated drops). Smaller marketplaces like Exchange.Art and Coral Cube will maintain niche positions. The bifurcation means investors will need accounts on both platforms — and understanding the royalty implications of each trade will remain essential.

3. Programmable Royalties and Smart Contracts

The next frontier is programmable royalties — NFTs with dynamic royalty rates that change based on conditions. For example, an NFT could have a 10% royalty for the first year, decreasing to 5% after that. Or royalties could be waived for holders who stake the NFT in a specific protocol. Solana's fast execution makes this type of dynamic logic feasible in ways it isn't on Ethereum.

4. Cross-Chain Royalty Standards

As Solana NFTs bridge to Ethereum and other chains via projects like Wormhole and Alliance Bridge, the royalty enforcement challenge becomes cross-chain. A Solana-native NFT traded on OpenSea (Ethereum) might not carry the same royalty enforcement. Expect new standards like CXIP and IERC-5218 to emerge for cross-chain royalty portability — but this is likely 2027 territory.

5. Regulatory Pressure

Regulators in the US and EU are beginning to examine NFT royalties. The EU's proposed Digital Asset Framework could classify NFT royalties as a form of securities fee, bringing them under regulatory oversight. In the US, the SEC has signaled interest in whether NFT royalties create an "investment contract" under the Howey Test. Any regulatory action could force marketplace policy changes across the board.

Bottom Line for Investors: The era of zero royalties on Solana is ending for new collections, but legacy collections will remain in a mixed enforcement environment for years. The smart investor strategy is to check royalty enforcement status before every purchase — and to factor royalty costs into your price targets, holding periods, and platform choices.

Practical Tips for Navigating Solana NFT Royalties as an Investor

Whether you are a newcomer or a seasoned Solana NFT trader, these practical guidelines will help you navigate the royalty landscape:

1
Check the Royalty Rate Before Buying

Before purchasing any NFT, look up its royalty configuration. On Magic Eden, the royalty rate is displayed in the listing details. On Tensor, you need to check the collection's metadata via Solscan or the Metaplex Token Metadata tool. Collections with 10%+ royalties should be evaluated differently from those with 0–5%.

2
Compare Prices Across Marketplaces

The same NFT can trade at different effective prices on Tensor versus Magic Eden because of royalty differences. A $1,000 NFT on Magic Eden with a 10% royalty costs $1,100 including royalty. On Tensor, the same NFT might be listed at $1,050 with no royalty. Use Tensor's aggregator or Hyperspace to compare prices across platforms before buying.

3
Factor Royalties Into Your Exit Strategy

If you buy an NFT on a royalty-enforcing marketplace, remember you will also pay the royalty when you sell. Calculate your break-even price including the buy-side and sell-side royalties. On a 10% royalty collection, a trade from $1,000 to $1,200 only nets you $190 profit (buy at $1,000 + $100 royalty = $1,100 cost; sell at $1,200 - $120 royalty = $1,080 received).

4
Prefer Enforced Royalty Collections for Long-Term Holds

Collections that enforce royalties signal committed creators with sustainable business models. For long-term holds (6+ months), prioritize enforced-royalty collections. The higher entry cost is offset by better community development and floor price support. For short-term trades (days to weeks), the royalty cost is a drag on returns.

5
Support Creators You Believe In

If you love a project and want to see it succeed, consider paying the full royalty even on platforms where it is optional. Every royalty payment signals to the marketplace that royalties matter — and creates economic pressure for better enforcement. On Tensor, adding a 5% creator tip costs you relatively little but makes a significant difference to the creator's revenue.

Frequently Asked Questions About Solana NFT Royalties

What is the standard royalty rate for Solana NFTs?

Most Solana NFT collections set royalty rates between 5% and 10%, represented as 500–1000 seller fee basis points in the Metaplex metadata. Blue-chip PFP collections typically use 5–7%, while generative art and 1/1 collections often use 7.5–10%.

Can I avoid paying royalties on Solana NFT purchases?

Yes — if the collection does not use Metaplex Core's hard royalty enforcement and you trade on a marketplace like Tensor that does not enforce royalties. However, collections with hard enforcement (Metaplex Core rule sets) cannot be traded without paying the full royalty on any compliant marketplace.

Do royalties affect NFT floor prices?

Yes. Floor prices on royalty-enforcing marketplaces tend to be 5–10% higher than on zero-royalty marketplaces because sellers factor in the royalty deduction. This creates a royalty premium that investors should account for when comparing prices across platforms.

Are royalties legally enforceable in court?

This is an unsettled legal question. On-chain royalty enforcement through Metaplex Core's rule sets is technically enforced by the blockchain program, not by courts. However, if a marketplace or aggregator intentionally bypasses on-chain enforcement, creators may have legal claims for tortious interference with contract — especially if the NFT's terms of sale explicitly mention royalties. No major test case has reached a final judgment as of 2026.

How do royalties work for compressed NFTs on Solana?

Compressed NFTs (cNFTs) — popular for gaming and mass-mint use cases — have a different royalty structure. Because compressed NFTs are stored in Merkle trees rather than individual accounts, the Metaplex royalty standard is not directly applicable. Most cNFT collections use 0% royalties, relying instead on primary sale revenue or in-ecosystem transaction fees.

Which marketplace is best for enforcing creator royalties on Solana?

Magic Eden offers the strongest creator royalty enforcement through its Royalty Shield program, covering over 4,000 collections. Exchange.Art is the best option for fine art and 1/1 artists, enforcing full royalties on all listings. For creators launching new collections, Magic Eden's creator dashboard provides the most comprehensive royalty tracking and payment tools.
